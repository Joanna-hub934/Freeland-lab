The Molecular Ecology of Invasive and Endemic Plants

Plants are essential to the functioning and sustainability of wetlands around the world, although they remain a relatively understudied group. We use a range of different tools including genetic analyses, and field and greenhouse experiments, to investigate how processes including reproduction (sexual and clonal), dispersal, local adaptation, and competition interact to inform the biodiversity of plant communities across different types of wetland habitats. Our investigations are most commonly on cattails (Typha spp.) although also include Phragmites australis, Sparganium spp., and Hippurus spp., in collaboration with researchers across North America, Europe, and China.

Hybridization and invasive wetland plants

Hybridization within and among species has been a powerful driver of evolutionary change across many plant taxa. We are interested in the ways in which hybridization can lead to highly competitive and often invasive wetland plants with the potential to transform ecosystems. We use a combination of field work, experiments and genetic analysis to investigate questions such as why hybridization between the same two species can lead to highly successful offspring in some areas, and much less successful hybrids in other areas.

Evolution and biogeography of circumarctic plants

Many aquatic plants have extremely broad distributions that span multiple continents. In collaboration with researchers in Russia and China, we are investigating the phylogeogeographic patterns of several taxonomic groups with the goal of inferring historical and ongoing patterns of dispersal and population establishment.

Conservation management

We work with natural resource managers in a number of different ways facilitate the connections between academic and applied research. These projects include monitoring native vs invasive vs hybrid Phragmites australis. We have also developed tools for detecting invasive plants, species-at-risk, and both plant and freshwater mussel communities using environmental DNA (eDNA) assays, which identify plants and animals to species without the need to observe or locate the organisms.
